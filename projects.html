Project 01

Data Selfie

Browser Extension | DATA X | 2017

What It Does

A Chrome/Firefox extension that tracks users while they browse Facebook to show them their own data traces. Collects the same information Facebook tracks‚Äîclicks, scroll times, post durations, typing activity‚Äîand uses personality prediction APIs (IBM Watson, Apply Magic Sauce) to show users what Facebook "sees." All data stored locally on user's computer.

Connection to YOU AGREED.

Makes invisible extraction visible ‚Äì Both projects reveal hidden data collection processes by transforming abstract tracking into concrete visualization. Creates visceral understanding through showing users their actual analysed data.

Privacy awareness through experience ‚Äì Users don't just read about data collection‚Äîthey see their actual data analysed with predicted personality traits, political orientation, and psychological profiles.

‚úì Local Processing Ethics
‚úó No Consent Challenge

Data Selfie proves that when surveillance is made visible and personal, people DO care about privacy‚Äîcontradicting the "privacy is dead" narrative. However, it's purely informational rather than provocative, missing the critical tension that makes YOU AGREED. more impactful.

Project 02

ToS;DR

Community Project | Hugo Roy et al. | 2012

What It Does

Community-driven project that analyses and grades Terms of Service from Grade A (best) to Grade E (worst). The project name plays on "Too Long; Didn't Read" and aims to fix "the biggest lie on the web"‚Äîthat people actually read terms before agreeing. Browser extension shows grade for current website with plain-language summaries.

Connection to YOU AGREED.

Directly addresses "I agree" problem ‚Äì Both projects acknowledge nobody reads terms and expose consent theater at the heart of digital services. Carnegie Mellon study: average user encounters 1,500 privacy policies/year, 2,500 words each‚Äîproving reading all terms is literally impossible.

Reveals buried clauses ‚Äì ToS;DR highlights problematic terms users unknowingly accept. YOU AGREED. buries Section 11 to prove this point experientially.
‚úì Validates Core Premise
‚úì Scale of Problem
‚úó Solution Not Critique
‚úó No Emotional Impact
Key Takeaway
ToS;DR validates YOU AGREED.'s core premise but attempts to solve the problem within existing frameworks rather than challenging whether "informed consent" is even possible for irreversible AI data extraction. YOU AGREED. goes further by demonstrating that even when violation is explained, most people still don't read carefully enough to avoid it.

Project 03

PIG 05049

Book/Installation | Christien Meindertsma | 2007

What It Does

Three years of research documenting all 185 products made from a single pig (number 05049) raised and slaughtered on a commercial farm in the Netherlands. Visual catalog shows everything from expected food items to surprising non-food products like ammunition, train brakes, cigarettes, bone china, and paint. Makes no moral judgment‚Äîpresents facts for reflection.

Connection to YOU AGREED.

Makes invisible processes visible ‚Äì PIG 05049 reveals industrial supply chains; YOU AGREED. reveals AI data extraction. Both use specificity for impact‚Äîtracking ONE thing completely to make abstract concrete.

Reveals disconnection ‚Äì Meindertsma proves "there are very many steps between the raw material and the end product... the knowledge gets lost." Same applies to gap between clicking "I agree" and AI model training.
‚úì Visibility Methodology
‚úì Specificity Creates Impact
‚úó Documentary Not Experiential
‚úó Observed Not Participated
Key Takeaway
Both projects share methodology: trace invisible processes to reveal how things work. YOU AGREED. applies this principle to cognitive data extraction‚Äîthe steps between clicking "I agree" and AI model training are so numerous and opaque that understanding is lost.

Project 04

Uninvited Guests

Short Film | Superflux | 2015

What It Does

4-minute speculative design film exploring frictions between elderly man Thomas and his smart home. Thomas receives smart devices from his children to monitor his diet, health, and sleep remotely. Film shows his escalating frustration as these "helpful" devices invade his autonomy, and his clever workarounds to deceive them. Uses comedy to make serious points about surveillance.

Connection to YOU AGREED.

Reveals surveillance disguised as care ‚Äì Uninvited Guests shows health monitoring = parental surveillance. YOU AGREED. shows AI assistance = cognitive extraction. Both explore how good intentions create problematic outcomes.

Makes invisible monitoring visible ‚Äì Bright colored objects symbolize "ghosts of the future"‚Äîphysical manifestations of invisible data streams.
‚úì Critical Design Approach
‚úì Tension Visualization
‚úó Fiction vs Actual Experience
‚úó Different Consent Context
Key Takeaway
Uninvited Guests shares YOU AGREED.'s critical design approach: creating speculative scenarios that reveal tensions in emerging technologies. However, Uninvited Guests focuses on IoT friction, while YOU AGREED. specifically targets the consent framework problem‚Äînot whether we can resist surveillance, but whether we ever truly consented to it.

Project 05

Open Informant

Wearable Badge | Superflux | 2013

What It Does

Wearable e-ink badge connected to smartphone app that searches communications for NSA surveillance trigger words (like "bomb," "assassination," but also "pork," "dock," "storm") and displays text fragments containing these words publicly on the badge worn on your body. Created in 2 weeks in response to NSA revelations. Open source‚Äîall designs and code available on GitHub.

Connection to YOU AGREED.

Reverses surveillance visibility ‚Äì Makes private data public to protest forced secrecy. "By openly displaying what is currently taken by forceful stealth, we question intrusive mass surveillance."

Protest through provocation ‚Äì Both use actual user data (not hypothetical) and let processing architecture reflect ethical position. Body as medium for making invisible visible.
‚úì Visibility as Critique
‚úì Uses Actual Data
‚úó Opt-in Protest Tool
‚úó State vs Corporate Surveillance
Key Takeaway
Open Informant and YOU AGREED. share methodology of "visibility as critique"‚Äîmaking hidden surveillance visible as protest. But Open Informant assumes users understand surveillance and provides tools for resistance. YOU AGREED. assumes users DON'T understand and proves this by replicating the exact consent-without-understanding pattern they fall for with ChatGPT.

What These Projects Reveal About YOU AGREED.

All five projects occupy the space where design meets activism, using experiential or visual methods to reveal hidden extraction mechanisms. They establish that surveillance capitalism exists, terms of service are unreadable, privacy visualization can raise awareness, and critical design can provoke reflection.

What they don't establish:

  • That consent frameworks themselves are broken for AI
  • That cognitive extraction is qualitatively different from behavioral tracking
  • That people need to FEEL the violation to understand it

This is YOU AGREED.'s contribution.

Not another project explaining surveillance or making tracking visible, but an intervention that demonstrates the fundamental inadequacy of current consent models when applied to irreversible cognitive data extraction‚Äîby replicating the exact violation in a way users can finally see and feel.

The difference between reading about surveillance and experiencing "YOUR DATA IS NOW UPLOADING... 10... 9... 8..."

That's the gap YOU AGREED. fills.
