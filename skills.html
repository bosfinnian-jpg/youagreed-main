ü§ñ AI-Assisted Development: The Methodological Irony

This project operates within the University of Leeds' "AI Green" assessment category, which explicitly encourages generative AI use as an integral component of the work. The category framework states: "In these assessments, you will demonstrate your ability to use Gen AI tools effectively and critically, to tackle complex problems, make informed judgments and generate creative solutions."


The methodological approach creates a deliberate tension: I am building a critique of AI data extraction using AI assistance to overcome technical limitations. This is not contradictory‚Äîit demonstrates the dual nature of these technologies and forms a critical element of the project's reflexive analysis.


Without AI assistance, this project would not exist. The technical implementation‚Äîinteractive data parsing, real-time analysis visualization, sophisticated countdown mechanics, responsive design patterns‚Äîexceeds my current coding capabilities. AI tools (specifically Claude and ChatGPT) enable me to translate conceptual vision into functional code, focusing my energy on what matters: the critical framework, user experience design, and educational impact.


What I am not doing: asking AI to generate my theoretical analysis, write my critical essay, or formulate my conceptual approach. The ideation, critical thinking, ethical framework, and creative direction remain entirely mine. AI serves as a technical translator, not an intellectual replacement.


The Reflexive Element: Using AI to critique AI surveillance creates a meta-layer of understanding. I benefit from this technology's democratizing potential‚Äîit grants me capabilities beyond my technical training. Simultaneously, I recognize that the same systems enabling my work operate through the consent theater mechanisms I'm trying to expose. This tension is productive, not problematic. It demonstrates that the question isn't "is AI beneficial or harmful?" but rather: "What are the terms of this exchange, do users understand those terms, and are current privacy frameworks adequate?"


My position: AI represents genuinely transformative capability. It should be used. But it cannot be allowed to replicate the surveillance capitalism model that Meta exemplifies‚Äîwhere extraction happens invisibly while utility happens visibly. Privacy regulations designed for cookie tracking are inadequate for AI model training, which involves permanent, irreversible cognitive pattern extraction. We need frameworks that acknowledge this qualitative difference.
