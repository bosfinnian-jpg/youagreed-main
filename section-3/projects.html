Data Selfie

Project 01

Browser Extension | DATA X | 2017

What It Does

A Chrome/Firefox extension that tracks users while they browse Facebook to show them their own data traces. Collects the same information Facebook tracks: clicks, scroll times, post durations, typing activity and then uses personality prediction APIs (IBM Watson, Apply Magic Sauce) to show users what Facebook sees. All data is stored locally on the user's computer.

Connection to YOU AGREED.

Makes invisible extraction visible. Both projects reveal hidden data collection processes by transforming abstract tracking into concrete visualisation. Creates understanding through showing users their actual analysed data.

Privacy awareness through experience. Users don't just read about their data collection; they see their actual data analysed with predicted personality traits, political orientation, and psychological profiles.

‚úì Local Processing Ethics
‚úó No Consent Challenge
‚úó Educational Not Provocative
Key Takeaway
Data Selfie proves that when surveillance is made visible and personal, people DO care about privacy, contradicting the "privacy is dead" narrative. However, it's purely informational rather than provocative, missing the critical tension that makes YOU AGREED. more impactful.

ToS;DR

Project 02

Community Project | Hugo Roy et al. | 2012

What It Does

Community-driven project that analyses and grades Terms of Service from Grade A (best) to Grade E (worst). The project name plays on "Too Long; Didn't Read" and aims to fix "the biggest lie on the web" that people actually read terms before agreeing. Browser extension shows grade for current website with plain-language summaries.

Connection to YOU AGREED.

Directly addresses "I agree" problem. Both projects acknowledge nobody reads terms and expose consent theatre at the heart of digital services. Carnegie Mellon study: average user encounters 1,500 privacy policies/year, 2,500 words each, proving reading all terms is literally impossible.

Reveals buried clauses. ToS;DR highlights problematic terms users unknowingly accept. YOU AGREED. buries Section 11 to prove this point experientially.
‚úì Validates Core Premise
‚úì Scale of Problem
‚úó Solution Not Critique
‚úó No Emotional Impact
Key Takeaway
ToS;DR validates YOU AGREED.'s core premise but attempts to solve the problem within existing frameworks rather than challenging whether "informed consent" is even possible for irreversible AI data extraction. YOU AGREED. goes further by demonstrating that even when violation is explained, most people still don't read carefully enough to avoid it.

PIG 05049

Project 03

Book/Installation | Christien Meindertsma | 2007

What It Does

Three years of research documenting all 185 products made from a single pig (number 05049) raised and slaughtered on a commercial farm in the Netherlands. Visual catalogue shows everything from expected food items to surprising non-food products like ammunition, train brakes, cigarettes, bone china, and paint. Makes no moral judgment, presents facts for reflection.

Connection to YOU AGREED.

Makes invisible processes visible. PIG 05049 reveals industrial supply chains; YOU AGREED. reveals AI data extraction. Both use specificity for impact, tracking ONE thing completely to make abstract concrete.

Reveals disconnection. Meindertsma proves "there are very many steps between the raw material and the end product... the knowledge gets lost." Same applies to gap between clicking "I agree" and AI model training.
‚úì Visibility Methodology
‚úì Specificity Creates Impact
‚úó Documentary Not Experiential
‚úó Observed Not Participated
Key Takeaway
Both projects share methodology: trace invisible processes to reveal how things work. YOU AGREED. applies this principle to cognitive data extraction, the steps between clicking "I agree" and AI model training are so numerous and opaque that understanding is lost.

Uninvited Guests

Project 04

Short Film | Superflux | 2015

What It Does

4-minute speculative design film exploring frictions between elderly man Thomas and his smart home. Thomas receives smart devices from his children to monitor his diet, health, and sleep remotely. Film shows his escalating frustration as these "helpful" devices invade his autonomy, and his clever workarounds to deceive them. Uses comedy to make serious points about surveillance.

Connection to YOU AGREED.

Reveals surveillance disguised as care. Uninvited Guests shows health monitoring = parental surveillance. YOU AGREED. shows AI assistance = cognitive extraction. Both explore how good intentions create problematic outcomes.

Makes invisible monitoring visible. Bright coloured objects symbolise "ghosts of the future", physical manifestations of invisible data streams.
‚úì Critical Design Approach
‚úì Tension Visualisation
‚úó Fiction vs Actual Experience
‚úó Different Consent Context
Key Takeaway
Uninvited Guests shares YOU AGREED.'s critical design approach: creating speculative scenarios that reveal tensions in emerging technologies. However, Uninvited Guests focuses on IoT friction, while YOU AGREED. specifically targets the consent framework problem, not whether we can resist surveillance, but whether we ever truly consented to it.

Open Informant

Project 05

Wearable Badge | Superflux | 2013

What It Does

Wearable e-ink badge connected to smartphone app that searches communications for NSA surveillance trigger words (like "bomb," "assassination," but also "pork," "dock," "storm") and displays text fragments containing these words publicly on the badge worn on your body. Created in 2 weeks in response to NSA revelations. Open source, all designs and code available on GitHub.

Connection to YOU AGREED.

Reverses surveillance visibility. Makes private data public to protest forced secrecy. "By openly displaying what is currently taken by forceful stealth, we question intrusive mass surveillance."

Protest through provocation. Both use actual user data (not hypothetical) and let the processing architecture reflect ethical position. Body as medium for making invisible visible.
‚úì Visibility as Critique
‚úì Uses Actual Data
‚úó Opt-in Protest Tool
‚úó State vs Corporate Surveillance
Key Takeaway
Open Informant and YOU AGREED. share methodology of "visibility as critique", making hidden surveillance visible as protest. But Open Informant assumes users understand surveillance and provides tools for resistance. YOU AGREED. assumes users DON'T understand and proves this by replicating the exact consent-without-understanding pattern they fall for with ChatGPT.

What These Projects Reveal About YOU AGREED.

The precedent projects demonstrate how design can expose hidden mechanisms of digital extraction, but they remain focused on behavioural tracking, visual awareness, and representational critique. What they do not address is the structural failure of current consent frameworks when applied to AI systems that extract cognitive data irreversibly. YOU AGREED. fills this gap by shifting from explanation to embodied experience: instead of showing surveillance, it makes users momentarily feel the violation through the countdown mechanic, revealing the inadequacy of traditional consent models in governing cognitive extraction.
