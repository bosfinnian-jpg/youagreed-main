Project Overview

The project explores the fundamental mismatch in how we currently regulate digital extraction and its roots from where it stemmed from. When surveillance capitalism first emerged through cookies and behavioural tracking, we built consent frameworks around a crucial assumption: reversibility. Data could be deleted. Tracking could be blocked. Users could opt out. These frameworks were inadequate even then dense privacy policies that nobody reads, pre-ticked boxes masquerading as choice, but at least the underlying model matched the technology. Behavioural data went into databases. Databases could be purged.

When you engage with ChatGPT, you're not generating a behavioural footprint like you do with clicks and timestamps. You're disclosing your reasoning processes, your emotional vulnerabilities, your problem-solving methods, your communication patterns and infinitely more. The system extracts not what you do, but how you think. The very infrastructure of cognition itself. And crucially, this extraction is permanent. Once your cognitive patterns train a model, they're transformed into mathematical weights distributed across millions of parameters. There is no delete button. There is no right to be forgotten. The model has learned from you, and that learning cannot be undone without destroying the model's fundamental capabilities.

Yet we continue applying the same broken consent theatre designed for cookie tracking to this irreversible cognitive extraction. The same dense terms of service. The same assumption that scrolling equals comprehension. The same fictional tale that "I Agree" constitutes informed decision-making. This isn't just an inadequate implementation of a sound frameworkit's a structural category error. Consent mechanisms built for reversible behavioural tracking cannot ethically govern permanent cognitive extraction, particularly when the conversational interface itself is designed to prompt disclosure. The empathetic tone, the human-like responses, the sense of helpful collaboration these aren't neutral features. They're mechanisms that manipulate users into revealing cognitive data they would never disclose through traditional interfaces.

You Agreed. makes this invisible harm experientially visible. The countdown creates authentic dread the feeling of violation when private data faces public exhibition. That feeling isn't artificial. It's recognition. The installation reveals that this violation is already occurring with every ChatGPT conversation, every Claude discussion, every AI interaction where you believe you're simply getting help while actually providing irreversible cognitive training data. The difference is that you couldn't see it before. The consent theatre functioned as intended: maintaining legal cover while users remained genuinely ignorant of what they were agreeing to. By collapsing the gap between legal consent and experiential understanding into a single moment, the project doesn't just critique surveillance capitalism; it demonstrates why Stage 1 frameworks categorically fail when applied to Stage 2 extraction, making abstract regulatory inadequacy feel as immediate and urgent as it actually is.

The Core Concept: By making people experience the simulated threat of public data exhibition through a 10-second countdown, the project reveals the gap between legal consent (clicking "I agree") and genuine informed consent (understanding what you're agreeing to). This visceral experience demonstrates that the violation is already happening with ChatGPT and other AI systems users just can't see it.
